Data Intelligence Platform

Enterprise teams across the organization were drowning in dashboards — 14 different views, each built by a different department, none talking to each other. Executives were making decisions based on whichever dashboard they opened last, not the one with the most accurate data.

I was brought in to unify the experience: one platform, one truth, designed for people who need answers fast but don't have time to learn a tool.

The Problem

The existing system was a patchwork of department-specific dashboards built over three years. Sales had their own. Operations had their own. Finance had their own. The problem wasn't the data — it was that nobody trusted anyone else's numbers because every dashboard told a slightly different story.

The real challenge was designing a single interface that could serve a VP checking quarterly revenue and an analyst drilling into hourly throughput — without making either feel like the tool wasn't built for them.

How I Approached It

  • Interviewed 8 stakeholders across 4 departments to map data consumption patterns
  • Audited all 14 existing dashboards for redundancy and usage frequency
  • Identified the 6 KPIs that actually drove decisions vs. the 40+ that were just noise
  • Ran card-sorting sessions to understand how different roles mentally group data

Design Direction

Instead of building another "everything dashboard," I designed a role-aware landing experience. The system detects who you are and surfaces the metrics that matter to your role first. Every view answers one question clearly before offering depth.

  • Progressive disclosure — summary first, drill-down on demand
  • Unified data language — consistent terminology and number formatting across all views
  • Contextual alerts — the dashboard tells you what changed, not just what is
  • Minimal chrome — data gets 90% of the screen, navigation gets 10%

What I Learned

The hardest part of dashboard design isn't visualization — it's deciding what not to show. Every stakeholder believes their metric is critical. The design work was as much about facilitation and alignment as it was about pixels. I learned that the best data interface is the one that makes the user forget they're using a tool and just start making decisions.
