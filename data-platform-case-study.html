AI Data Platform

Fourteen disconnected dashboards. Three different teams. Zero shared context. I designed a unified AI-powered analytics platform that turned fragmented data silos into a single source of truth — with predictive insights that tell you what's about to happen, not just what already did.

Lead UX Designer I Spatial Techno Solutions Jan 2026–Present Team of 8 (2 Designers) AI / Enterprise Analytics

Context

The organization relied on 14 separate dashboards across three departments — operations, planning, and executive leadership. Each dashboard was built by a different vendor, used a different data model, and told a different version of the truth. Leadership couldn't answer basic questions like "are we on track this quarter?" without pulling data from four systems and reconciling it manually in Excel.

I was brought in to design a single, unified analytics platform that would consolidate all 14 data sources into one AI-augmented decision-making environment — with role-based views that serve analysts, managers, and executives without overwhelming any of them.

The Problem

The surface-level problem was obvious: too many dashboards. But the deeper problem was that each dashboard was designed for a different mental model.

  • Operations dashboards showed real-time metrics — uptime, throughput, error rates — but had no trend lines or forecasting. They answered "what's happening now?" but not "is this normal?"
  • Planning dashboards showed historical trends but were always 2-3 weeks behind. By the time leadership saw a problem, it was already a crisis
  • Executive dashboards were high-level summaries that masked important nuances. A green KPI could hide three failing sub-metrics. Leadership felt informed but wasn't
  • No shared vocabulary. "Active users" meant something different in every dashboard. "Revenue" was calculated three different ways
  • Analysts spent 60% of their time preparing data rather than analyzing it — pulling exports, cleaning formats, reconciling discrepancies

Executive view: unified KPIs, AI-driven anomaly flags, and predictive trend lines — one screen, complete picture

Research & Discovery

  • Dashboard audit — Catalogued all 14 dashboards: their data sources, update frequencies, user bases, and overlapping metrics. Found that 60% of widgets across dashboards displayed variations of the same 8 core metrics
  • User shadowing — Spent 2 days with analysts, 1 day with operations managers, and 1 day with a VP. Documented their complete data consumption workflow — from opening their first dashboard to making a decision
  • Pain point mapping — Identified 12 distinct friction points. The top 3: "I don't trust this number" (data inconsistency), "I can't find what I need" (scattered across systems), "By the time I see it, it's too late" (no predictive capability)
  • Competitive analysis — Studied Looker, Metabase, Mixpanel, and Amplitude's information architectures to understand how they handle role-based data density

The most revealing finding: when I asked the VP "what decision did your last dashboard help you make?", she paused for 15 seconds and said "I think it confirmed something I already suspected." The dashboards weren't driving decisions — they were confirming biases. That's a fundamentally different design problem.

Design Process

Architecture: Three Layers, One Platform

Instead of building one dashboard that tried to serve everyone, I designed three progressive density layers within the same platform — each serving a different cognitive mode:

  • Pulse View (Executives) — 6 KPIs maximum. AI-flagged anomalies surface only when something deviates from expected range. Green doesn't mean "good" — it means "within predicted bounds." Red means "this is outside what the model expected." This forces attention on genuine signals, not noise
  • Analysis View (Managers) — Full trend lines, comparison tools, drill-down capabilities. Every metric links to its source data. Hovering on any number shows calculation methodology — building trust through transparency
  • Explorer View (Analysts) — Raw query builder, custom visualization creator, data export pipeline. Analysts can build their own views without engineering support — and share them with managers as saved "lenses"

AI Integration — Not a Gimmick

AI wasn't bolted on as a feature — it was woven into the decision-making fabric:

  • Anomaly Detection — The system learns baseline patterns per metric and flags deviations before they become visible on charts. A 3% drop that would take a human analyst 2 days to notice gets flagged in real-time
  • Predictive Trend Lines — Every chart shows not just "what happened" but "what's likely to happen in the next 7/14/30 days" based on historical patterns and current trajectory
  • Natural Language Summaries — Each dashboard section generates a plain-English summary: "Revenue is trending 4% below Q3 forecast, primarily driven by a 12% drop in Region B. This is consistent with seasonal patterns from 2024 but 2x the expected magnitude"

Solution

The shipped platform delivers a single, unified analytics environment where the same underlying data powers three progressively complex views:

  • Unified Data Model — All 14 data sources feed into one canonical schema. "Active users" means one thing everywhere. "Revenue" is calculated one way. Trust is built through consistency
  • Role-Based Density — Executives see 6 KPIs with AI annotations. Managers see full dashboards with drill-down. Analysts see raw data with query tools. Same data, different cognitive loads
  • AI-First Insights — Anomaly detection, predictive forecasting, and natural language summaries are not separate features — they're embedded into every chart, every KPI card, every view
  • Shared Context — When an executive flags a KPI for investigation, the analyst gets the exact same view plus the drill-down tools. No more "can you send me that dashboard?" — context travels with the data

Key Decision

The hardest decision was rejecting the "single configurable dashboard" approach. The stakeholders initially wanted one dashboard where users could toggle complexity with filters and settings. We prototyped it.

The result was a Frankenstein: executives accidentally enabled analyst-level detail and couldn't find the toggle to go back. Analysts turned off "simple mode" and lost their custom queries. The configurability created more confusion than the 14 separate dashboards had.

The three-view architecture was counter-intuitive — it felt like building three dashboards instead of one. But each view was purpose-built for its audience's cognitive mode: scanning (executives), comparing (managers), exploring (analysts). The result was dramatically simpler for everyone.

Constraint

The 14 source dashboards couldn't be decommissioned during the transition. Some had regulatory reporting dependencies — government reports were generated directly from dashboard exports. This meant the new platform had to produce identical exports to the legacy systems while presenting the data completely differently.

I designed an "export compatibility layer" that preserved exact legacy formatting while the UI presented the same data in the new unified schema. Users see the improved interface but can still generate legacy-format reports for compliance — no disruption to regulatory workflows.

What I Learned

The biggest lesson was that dashboard consolidation isn't a visual design problem — it's an epistemological one. Different people need to know different things at different levels of certainty. A VP needs to know "are we on track?" with 80% confidence. An analyst needs to know "why did metric X change on Tuesday?" with 99% certainty. These aren't the same question, and they can't be answered by the same interface.

The AI integration taught me that the most powerful AI features are invisible. Users don't think "the AI flagged this anomaly" — they think "the dashboard told me something important." When the technology disappears into the experience, that's when it's working.

The best analytics platform doesn't give you more data — it gives you fewer, better decisions. When a VP opens the platform, sees one red flag, understands why in 10 seconds, and knows what to do in 30 — that's the design working.
